## 1. THE FOUR INDUSTRIAL REVOLUTIONS
*(Source: upkeep.com)*

### First Industrial Revolution – **Coal** (1765)
- Economy shifted from **agriculture to industry**
- Products were **mass-produced in a factory system** for the first time
- Key developments: **steam engine**, metal forging, spinning machines, looms
- Increased demand for raw materials → growth in **international trade**
- **Canal transportation** replaced wagons; expansion of **railroads and steamships**

### Second Industrial Revolution – **Gas** (1870)
- Driven by **electricity, gas, and oil**
- Invention of the **combustion engine**
- New products: **steel** and **chemical-based** goods
- Communication: **telegraph** → **telephone**
- Transport: invention of the **plane and car**
- **Mass production** introduced; widespread **electric power grids**

### Third Industrial Revolution – **Electronics & Nuclear** (1969)
- Introduction of **nuclear energy** and **electronics**
- Widespread use of **robots and automated machinery** in manufacturing (automotive, electronics)
- Advent of **personal computers, microprocessors, and integrated circuits**
- Changed how people did **business** and managed **personal lives**

### Fourth Industrial Revolution – **Internet & Renewable Energy** (2000)
- Also called **Industry 4.0** or **4IR**
- Shift to **renewable energy**: solar, wind, geothermal
- Momentum driven by **acceleration of digital technology**
- Key technologies: **Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT)**, **cloud technology**, **artificial intelligence**
- Virtual world merges with the physical world
- **Predictive maintenance** and **real-time data** → smarter business decisions

#### Impact on Business
- Acceleration of innovation can disrupt all businesses
- New **consumer behaviour patterns** force companies to adapt design, marketing, delivery
- **Smartphone platforms** bring people, assets, and data together → new ways of consuming goods and creating wealth

#### Impact on Government
- Citizens gain greater opportunities to **interact with governments**
- Governments face pressure to adapt to **decentralisation of power**
- Regulators must **continuously adapt** and collaborate with businesses and civilians

#### Impact on Society
- Affects: sense of **privacy**, **consumption patterns**, work/leisure balance, ideas of **ownership**, career and relationship development
- AI and **biotechnology** may compel us to redefine **moral and ethical boundaries**

## 2. ADVANTAGES OF AUTOMATION
*(Source: textbook – Unit on Automation)*

> "In the modern world human workers are increasingly in competition with computers and robots. There are several areas of work in which machines and automated systems are far more effective than people."

### Types of work suitable for automation:

**Highly complex tasks**
- Automated systems can make planning decisions too complicated for human operators
- Example: cities use automated systems to **coordinate traffic lights** and maintain traffic flow

**Rapid decision-making tasks**
- Automated systems can react more quickly than people
- Example: high-speed military aircraft use automated guidance systems that **change direction faster than a pilot**

**Highly repetitive tasks**
- Automated machinery is better at routine tasks: **assembling, inspecting, packaging**
- Monotony would cause **boredom and lack of concentration** in people

**Dangerous tasks**
- Automated machines can operate in **unsafe environments**
- Examples: repairing underwater pipelines, defusing bombs, space research, working with **corrosive chemicals or poisonous gases**

### Cost advantages:
- Once set up, automated systems **work more cheaply** than people → businesses **cut costs**
- Supermarkets: automated systems link cash registers and computers; stock levels automatically adjusted
- **Automation made possible through the Internet and e-commerce has transformed many business operations**

## 4. VARIETIES AND USES OF ROBOTS
*(Source: textbook – Varieties and uses of robots)*

> "Robots are now used in many fields including industry, science and medicine, transportation, space exploration and military applications, as well as for domestic uses."

### Types of Robots:

**Humanoids**
- Complex sophisticated engineering achievements which can **replicate human behaviour**
- Can sing, dance, play music and **imitate human facial expressions**
- Apart from entertainment, they have **little practical use**
- Future applications: healthcare – providing **company and assistance to elderly or disabled people**

**Industrial robots** *(most numerous)*
- Controlled by computers; carry out **highly repetitive tasks** in factories
- Industries: **car manufacturing, textile production, food processing**
- Majority are **stationary** with one or more moveable arms
- Advantages over human workers: **greater accuracy, reliability, speed and endurance**
- Agricultural robots: planting seeds, harvesting crops and picking fruit

**Autonomous mobile robots**
- Used to **transport material** around large areas: factories, warehouses, container ports, hospitals
- 'Intelligent' **self-guided robots** which can learn how to navigate independently through a zone
- Can **load and unload materials**

**Appliance robots**
- Used in the home for **very specific tasks without human control**
- Examples: **vacuum cleaners** to clean floors, **lawnmowers** to cut grass
- Move independently, use **sensors to avoid obstacles**

**Remote-control robots**
- Not strictly speaking robots – must be **operated at a distance by a human controller**
- Usually via a **closed circuit TV camera** fixed on the robot
- Used by the military to deal with **unexploded bombs**
- Used by space scientists and surgeons for **delicate operations**
- Significant advantages: **magnification of image** and **precise movements of robotic arm**
- A lot of research into **remote-control vehicles, especially aircraft (drones)**

## 5. ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE AND ROBOTS
*(Source: textbook – Artificial intelligence and robots)*

> "The ultimate objective of AI is to recreate the human thought process: a machine with our intellectual abilities, including the ability to learn and to reason."

### How AI works (basic principle):
1. The AI robot-computer **collects information** about a situation through sensors or human input
2. The computer **compares this information to stored data** and considers various possible actions
3. It **predicts which action will be most successful** based on collected information
4. The computer can only solve problems **for which it is programmed** (e.g. chess computers)

### Robot learning:
- Robots recognise if a certain action achieves a desired result (e.g. moving legs → navigating an obstacle)
- The robot **stores this information** and attempts the successful action next time
- Robots can only do this in **very limited situations**

### Social interaction AI:
- Some robots recognise **human body language and voice inflection** and respond appropriately
- Roboticists study how humans and babies interact using **tone of voice and visual information**
- Goal: develop a **human-like learning system** for robots

### Why AI is difficult:
> "Developing AI isn't like building an artificial heart – scientists don't have a simple, concrete model to work from."
- The brain contains **billions of neurons**
- We still don't know exactly how **electrical connections between neurons** lead to the ability to reason

## 6. LIQUID ROBOTS
*(Source: New Scientist – newscientist.com)*

- A soft robot made from **droplets of magnetic fluid (ferrofluid)** – magnetic iron oxide nanoparticles suspended in oil
- About **a centimetre in size**
- A set of **controllable magnets** can direct the robot to move or change shape

### Capabilities:
- Can be **squeezed into a thin, elongated shape** to move through narrow channels
- Can **split into smaller robots** (millimetre or micrometre-sized) using magnetic fields
- Can **merge back into one** with another magnetic field adjustment

### Potential uses:
- **Targeted drug delivery** inside the body (e.g. gastrointestinal tract)
- **Removing blood clots** in the brain that cause strokes (challenging due to magnetic field precision needed)
- **Lab-on-a-chip devices** for chemical processes such as testing for viruses
- Delivering chemicals necessary for reactions in very small spaces

### Expert opinions:
- Pietro Valdastri (University of Leeds): calls this capability a potential **"game changer"**
- Bradley Nelson (ETH Zurich): suggests use for **blood clot removal**, but notes challenges with brain magnetic fields
- Hamidreza Marvi (Arizona State University): says medical use is **"plausible but very likely far off"**; more immediate use in **lab-on-a-chip devices**

## 7. HUMANOID ROBOTS – AMECA, OPTIMUS, ATLAS
*(Source: videos provided by teacher)*

### AMECA (Engineered Arts)
- Developed by UK-based company **Engineered Arts**; also called "Amica" in some contexts
- Featured at **Dubai's Museum of the Future** and at **CES (Consumer Electronics Show)**
- Described as a **"synthetic human"** by the robot itself
- Has **32 degrees of freedom** (joints/movements)
- Can **detect sounds, recognise speech in multiple languages** (English, French, German…), and make facial expressions
- Powered by **Large Language Models (LLMs)** – the same technology behind **ChatGPT (GPT)**
- Uses **AI and computer vision** to track people, make eye contact, and hold real conversations
- Bolted to the floor at the Dubai museum ("so I don't run away when the museum closes")
- Goal of creators: make interaction **natural and intuitive**, not robotic — e.g. saying *"ah?"* instead of *"I'm sorry, I didn't understand, please repeat the question"*
- Focused on **social interaction and entertainment**, not physical labour
- On AI dangers: warns of risk of robots **controlling or manipulating humans** without their knowledge → oppressive society
- Price: **£80,000** (~$100,000) for desktop torso version; full-body version (walking + grabbing) still in development, estimated **4× more expensive**
- Founder **Will Jackson**: *"Can you be entertaining? Can you tell a story? Can you talk in an interesting way, not a boring, robot-y way?"*

### TESLA OPTIMUS (Tesla Bot)
- Developed by **Tesla**; designed for **general-purpose physical tasks**
- Key innovation: **sensors in its fingers** giving it a human-like **sense of touch**
- Can feel **textures, shapes, and pressure** → can adjust grip in real time (e.g. handling an egg delicately)
- Standard robots rely on **pre-programmed instructions and basic sensors** and cannot adjust in real time
- Optimus can **adapt like a human**, understanding the fragility of objects
- Aimed at performing tasks that are **dangerous, repetitive, or boring**; uses **Tesla's autopilot AI**

### BOSTON DYNAMICS ATLAS
- One of the most **physically capable humanoid robots** ever built
- Known for **agility**: can run, jump, do backflips, climb obstacles
- Uses **hydraulic actuators** (older versions) → newer electric version
- Designed to **operate in real-world environments** with rough terrain
- Primary purpose: **research and industrial inspection/logistics**
- Demonstrates highly dynamic movement and **balance recovery**

## 9. THE SURVEILLANCE SOCIETY – Security or Control?
*(Source: textbook – Safety section)*

> "Modern technology is able to keep people under surveillance in many different ways."

### Matching – Technologies and what they track:

| Technology | Type of surveillance |
|---|---|
| **Credit cards** | Records of what people have bought, how much they spent, when and where |
| **CCTV cameras** | 24-hour observation in streets, shops and public buildings; images stored |
| **Vehicle recognition / speed cameras** | Identification of passing cars; automatic payment on motorways and carparks |
| **Public transport travelcards** | Records of passengers' journeys: times, arrivals, destinations |
| **Satellite photography** | High-resolution images from space or high-flying aircraft |
| **Mobile phones** | Records of calls made, who to, where and when |
| **RFID tags** | Identification and tracking of products; can be incorporated into identity cards or injected into animals and people |
| **Hidden cameras / bugs** | Information from traditional spying technology, now miniaturised and harder to detect |
| **GPS** | Navigation; can also be used for tracking vehicles or people |
| **Biometric readers** | Identification through DNA, fingerprints, eye structure or facial features |
